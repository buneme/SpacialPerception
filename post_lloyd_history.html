History of autonomous vehicles

How autonomous vehicles rose to be a game-changing technology

By Lloyd

Defence Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) Grand Challenge

2004

In 2004, DARPA held their first Grand Challenge. It offered $1 million for whoever could make a car capable of driving 150 miles through the Mojave Desert the fastest.

None of the competitors made it to the finish line, however it made people realise that self-driving cars were possible.

2005

The next one was in 2005, where 5 successfully completed the course. The Stanford racing team was in first place with a time of 6 hours and 54 minutes.

Google

In 2010, Google (now a spin-off company called Waymo) announced that it had secretly had employees (some of whome competed in DARPA's challenges) working on making a system for self-driving cars with the goal of launching a commericial vehicle by 2020. They hoped it would half the number of car accidents each year.

A video released from Waymo showing off thier self-driving technology

By 2015, they had logged 1 million miles without causing an accident. In 2016, one of their cars was the first from the company to cause an accident. It attempted to avoid sandbags blocking its path while moving forward towards a stoplight. In doing so, it struck the side of a bus.

In addition, Waymo has lobbied for and got legislation passed to make autonomous cars street legal in four states.

Other competitors

By 2013, many car manufacturers, like GM, Ford, Mercedes Benz, Volkswagen, Audi, Nissan, Toyota, BMW and Volvo had begun testing driverless car systems and have been doing so since.

Tesla

In October 2014, Tesla announced its first version of AutoPilot, which came equipped with some Model S cars. It was capable of lane control with autonomous steering, braking and speed limit adjustment based on signals image recognition.

The first fatal accident involving a self-driving car was in Florida in May 2016. While a Tesla Model S was in AutoPilot mode, it failed to apply the brakes when a 18-wheel tractor-trailer made a left turn in front of the Tesla at an intersection on a non-controlled access highway.

Tesla ended its partnership with Mobileye, who was in charge of making the vision-on-a-chip system that processes and analyses images to enable vechile and lane detection for Tesla cars, so that they could work on their on radar system by themselves.

Delphi Automative

In April 2015, Delphi Automative became the first company to complete a coast-to-coast journey across the USA with an automated vehicle, travelling from San Francisco to New York under computer control for 99% of the journey.

nuTonomy

In August 2016, Singapore launched the first self-driving car taxi service, provided by nuTonomy (which was bought by Delphi Automative late last year).

General Motors

In January this year, General Motors (GM) - known as one of the leaders in autonomous vehicle technology - announced that it plans to make an autonomous car without a steering wheel or pedals by 2019. You can read more about it here.
