SLAM - limitations, saftey and reinforcement learning

All about how SLAM uses the gathered data, along with some limitations, saftey issues, and more

By Buneme Mon 5 March
Stands for Simultaneous Localisation and Mapping Technically, SLAM doesn’t refer to a specific algorithm or software, but its just the problem of trying to do these 2 things at the same time: Localisation: work out the position/orientation of an object using sensors and navigate environment Mapping the structure of the environment that the robot is in For these reasons SLAM is most useful when the robot needs to be 100% autonomous, GPS is unavailable, or when there is no prior knowledge about the environment Examples of such a situation would be autonomous planetary exploration, subsea autonomous vehicles, autonomous airborne vehicles, and autonomous vehicles used in tasks like mining The optical sensors are 1D (single beam) 2D (sweeping laser rangefinders) or 3D High definition LiDar (these are just examples). These sensors are used to extract landmark information. Tactile sensors (sensing by touch) can also be used to extract landmark data but these only contain information about points that are very close to the object. Most practical SLAM tasks involve a mixture of visual and tactile sensors
